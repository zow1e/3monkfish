# Holland Lop — general pet-care orientation (MVP seed)

> **Disclaimer:** This text is for general education only. It is not veterinary advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Always consult a qualified rabbit-savvy veterinarian for medical decisions.

## Breed overview

Holland Lops are a small lop-eared rabbit breed, often kept as companion animals. They typically have a compact body and friendly temperament when socialized. Individual rabbits vary widely in personality regardless of breed.

## Housing and environment

Provide a spacious, predator-safe enclosure with room to stretch, hop, and stand fully upright on hind legs. Solid flooring or comfortable mats help protect feet; wire-only floors can contribute to sore hocks. Keep the area quiet, well-ventilated, and away from extreme heat. Rabbits are sensitive to heat stress; cool shade and fresh water matter especially in warm climates.

## Diet basics

The foundation diet for healthy adult pet rabbits is high-quality grass hay (timothy or other appropriate hays), available at all times. Fresh water should always be accessible. Limited measured pellets formulated for rabbits and a small amount of leafy greens can complement hay, but introduce new greens gradually. Avoid sudden large diet changes. Treats should be occasional and not replace hay.

## Grooming and coat

Holland Lops have a dense coat that benefits from regular gentle brushing, especially during seasonal shedding. Check around the tail and hindquarters for soiling; keep the area clean and dry. Long nails should be trimmed carefully or by a professional if you are not trained. Never bathe a rabbit like a dog; spot cleaning is usually safer if needed.

## Behavior and enrichment

Rabbits need daily exercise, hiding places, and safe chew toys. They are prey species and may startle easily; predictable routines and gentle handling build trust. Provide digging boxes or safe cardboard shredding as enrichment. Bonded pairs or compatible companions can improve welfare when housing permits, but introductions must be done carefully.

## Digestive health signals

A healthy rabbit eats and produces fecal pellets regularly. Reduced appetite, small or absent fecal output, bloating, or lethargy can be emergencies in rabbits. If you notice these signs, seek prompt veterinary care from a rabbit-experienced vet.

## Dental awareness

Rabbits have teeth that grow continuously. Appropriate hay consumption supports natural wear. Signs such as drooling, dropping food, weight loss, or selective eating warrant a veterinary dental evaluation.

## Common concerns (non-diagnostic)

**Shedding:** Increased hair ingestion during molts can contribute to hair in the digestive tract; brushing and hay-forward diets are common supportive measures—ask your vet if you see reduced appetite or abnormal stools.

**Ear care:** Lop ears can have reduced air circulation; keep ears clean and dry and learn what is normal for your rabbit so you notice discharge, odor, or head tilt early.

**Urinary habits:** Changes in urination posture, straining, or blood in urine are reasons to call a vet promptly.

## When to see a vet urgently

Seek immediate rabbit-savvy veterinary care for: difficulty breathing, collapse, seizures, severe lethargy, not eating or not passing stool for roughly 12 hours (sooner for infants or if pain is suspected), suspected toxin exposure, trauma, uncontrolled bleeding, or a painfully distended abdomen.

## Preventive care

Establish a relationship with a rabbit-knowledgeable veterinarian for annual wellness checks, vaccination where recommended by your vet and local law, and parasite prevention tailored to your region and lifestyle.
